### üåç Part 1: What is a Network?

At its core, a **network** is fundamentally about **connection**. It's a system where multiple, separate entities are linked together. A relatable example is a social circle, where individuals are connected through shared interests, hobbies, or backgrounds.

This concept of interconnected systems isn't limited to technology. We can see networks all around us:

*   The public transit routes that connect a city.
*   The national power grid that distributes electricity.
*   The community of neighbors interacting with each other.
*   The postal service responsible for moving mail between locations.

When we talk about networking in the context of computing, we're applying this same principle to electronic devices. It's the reason your phone can access information from all over the world. The study of networking explores how these devices establish communication and the **protocols** that ensure this communication is orderly and reliable.

A digital network can be as small as two devices or as vast as the internet, which connects billions. These network **nodes** can be anything from standard computers and smartphones to more specialized equipment like security cameras, smart traffic lights, or even automated agricultural systems.
